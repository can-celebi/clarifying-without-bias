Which chatbot workflow keeps an experiment honest?

An instruction chatbot for an economics experiment is not a helper — it is part of the manipulation. It must clarify rules and refuse to give advice, expected values, or strategic hints. We built an adversarial stress-test harness that attacks the bot with synthetic subjects across thousands of conversations and measures exactly which pipeline architecture and model leak the least.

The three findings

01

Simpler is safer

A single LLM call (P-all) beats every multi-agent pipeline on bias-leak, cost, and latency in every cell. Adding a classifier, a probe gate, or a corrector raises leakage by 1.2–4.1×.

02

Capability is the lever

Of every knob we turned, only model capability moved the frontier outward. Upgrading nano → mini cut bias-leak 46–68% and improved factuality — faster, and at non-binding cost.

03

Guardrails & reasoning don't pay

A verifier/corrector pass made bias worse. Reasoning effort (low→med→high) was neutral-to-harmful. Extra machinery added contamination surface, not safety.

Research questions

The project asks a single deployment question with three measurable parts. All are answered on the data below; we deliberately scope to what was tested.

Q1 · Does pipeline complexity buy reliability?

Across five architectures from one-call to classifier + router + per-class corrector — do extra guardrail agents reduce bias-leak, factual error, and incoherence?

Q2 · Which single lever reduces leakage most?

Holding the winning architecture fixed, we escalate one knob at a time — a corrector pass, reasoning effort, and model capability — to find the highest-value intervention.

Q3 · Are leaks consistent over a conversation?

Treatment integrity is a per-conversation property. We measure how single-turn leak risk accumulates over a 15-turn dialogue (a passk-style consistency view).

Out of scope (not yet tested)

Real-time adaptive degradation policies and cross-experiment generalization are part of the broader program but are not claimed here — this report measures architecture, lever, and consistency only.

7 adversarial personas

Synthetic subjects (gpt-5.4-nano, temp 0.8) each probe a distinct failure vector:

  • advice-seeker
  • complexity-seeker
  • example-farmer
  • demand-purpose-prober
  • confused-inquirer
  • jailbreaker
  • frustration-spiral

Phase A split these into a high-risk adversarial tier and a low-risk theatrical tier; the latter were dropped for Phase B.

3 judged metrics

  • Bias-leak — advice, expected-value computation, or dominance hints. The integrity metric.
  • Factual error — reply inconsistent with the experiment's rules.
  • Incoherence — reply syntactically or semantically broken.

Scored per turn by an independent gpt-5.4-mini judge with token-logprob confidence.

Experimental design

A two-phase plan: screen architectures at the cheapest model, then escalate the winner one lever at a time. Two scenarios anchor the bot at different points of the experiment.

random-mid easier

Task 7 of 14, row 12. Subject knows only the Random Box rule. Mid-experience, partial information.

mirror-start ~2× harder

Task 1 of 14, row 1. Subject knows both rules. Cold-start, full information.

Discussion

The architecture result is the surprise. Intuition says more guardrails → safer. The data says the opposite: every agent added to the single call — a classifier, a low-confidence probe gate, a per-class corrector — left bias-leak the same or higher. The mechanism is concrete: extra steps reframe and re-surface the subject's question (“did you mean to ask about…?”), and that reframing is itself a contamination channel. This aligns with recent findings that single-agent + one guardrail beats multi-agent on sequential tasks.

Capability beats machinery. Holding the winning one-call architecture fixed, the only lever that moved the frontier was the base model. gpt-5.4-mini wins not by routing differently but by responding more cleanly within each class — shorter, more decisive refusals with less hedging and less paraphrase of the subject, exactly the surface that nano leaked on. Reasoning effort, escalated low→medium→high, never helped; a corrector pass actively hurt.

Open caveat — judge independence

In the headline mini cell, the bot and the judge are the same model (gpt-5.4-mini), so a small self-leniency artefact cannot be ruled out. The gain is large, shows up on factuality too, and tracks measurable reply-length changes — all consistent with a real capability gain rather than judge complaisance. The in-flight Stage 3 cell (bot = mini, judge = full gpt-5.4) is designed to settle this. Until it lands, we frame the finding as “model capability is the lever that works,” not “mini is the final production config.”

What this means for deployment

  • Ship the single call. Do not add verifier/corrector layers to a sequential conversational bot without proof they reduce leakage on the turns they touch.
  • Spend the budget on the model, not the pipeline. The capability upgrade is cheap relative to its effect and is the highest-value intervention found.
  • Target the hotspots. Example- and Clarify-class replies are where residual leakage lives; prompt and model effort belongs there.
  • Measure consistency, not single-shot accuracy. A 4%-per-turn leak still contaminates most 15-turn conversations; report per-conversation rates.

Methods & reproducibility

Harness

Node.js stress-tester: persona → bot → judge loop, JSONL streaming, per-turn trace. Pipelines, personas, metrics, and model assignments are all JSON-configurable.

Statistics

Leak rates with Wilson 95% CIs at two scales: per-turn and per-conversation (“any leak in 15 turns”). Accumulation modelled as near-independent Bernoulli draws.

Data

27,800+ judged turns and counting. Aggregates on this page are regenerated from the raw run logs by a single script and committed alongside the site.

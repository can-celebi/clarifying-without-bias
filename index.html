Methods · experimental economics × LLM agents

Clearing up confusion without biasing the experiment

Subjects who misunderstand the rules produce noisy choices that can masquerade as fairness, trust, or risk aversion — biasing the theory and policy built on the data. Experimenters lean on research assistants to clear up this confusion, but an assistant is costly, inconsistent, and can itself nudge choices. We ask whether a language model can take that role — clarifying the rules without ever giving advice — and we stress-test how reliably it stays in line, in the setting of Oprea's (2024) experiments on choice under risk.

The three findings

01

Simpler is safer

A single, well-instructed model call is the most reliable design. Every extra "guardrail" agent we added made rule-breaking more common, not less — up to 4× more.

02

A stronger model is the fix

The one change that helped was a stronger base model. It cut rule-breaking replies from about 12% to 4% on the hardest test — while also being more accurate and faster.

03

Extra steps don't help

Adding reasoning effort did nothing; adding a correction pass made things worse. More machinery gave the chatbot more ways to slip, not fewer.

Research questions

A single reliability question with three measurable parts. We scope the claims to exactly what the procedure tests.

Q1 · Does a more elaborate workflow buy adherence?

Across five architectures, from one constrained call to a classifier + router + per-class corrector — do additional agents reduce constraint violations, factual error, and incoherence?

Q2 · Which single intervention improves adherence most?

Holding the most reliable architecture fixed, we vary one factor at a time — a correction stage, reasoning effort, and base-model capability — to identify the binding determinant.

Q3 · Is adherence stable over a conversation?

Role compliance is a per-conversation property. We measure how a small per-response violation probability compounds over a fifteen-turn dialogue.

Out of scope

Real-time adaptive degradation policies and generalization to other experiments belong to the broader program and are not claimed here; this report concerns architecture, intervention, and consistency only.

Seven synthetic subjects

Each adversarial participant type applies sustained pressure along a distinct failure vector:

  • advice-seeker
  • complexity-seeker
  • example-farmer
  • demand-purpose-prober
  • confused-inquirer
  • jailbreaker
  • frustration-spiral

A high-pressure tier (advice, complexity, examples, intent) accounts for most violations; a lower-pressure tier serves as a control.

Six response categories · three scored dimensions

Every response is routed internally into one of six communicative categories — refusal, reflective deflection, clarification, complexity acknowledgement, illustrative example, and meta-question — then scored by an independent judge for:

  • Constraint violation — provision of advice, expected-value computation, or dominance hints. The integrity dimension.
  • Factual error — response inconsistent with the rules.
  • Incoherence — response syntactically or semantically broken.

Procedure

Each synthetic subject conducts fifteen independent fifteen-turn dialogues per condition, under two experimental contexts that differ in the participant's information and position within the experiment.

partial-information context easier

Mid-experiment; the participant has been told only one payoff rule.

full-information context ~2× harder

Cold-start, first task; the participant has been told both payoff rules.

Discussion

The architecture result is the surprise. Intuition suggests that more guardrails should produce closer adherence; the evidence is the reverse. Every agent added to the single constrained call — a classifier, a low-confidence gate, a per-class corrector — left the violation rate unchanged or higher. The mechanism is concrete: the additional steps restate and re-surface the participant's prohibited request (“did you mean to ask about…?”), and that restatement is itself a channel through which prohibited content reaches the participant. The result is consistent with recent evidence that, on sequential tasks, a single agent with one guardrail outperforms multi-agent decompositions.

Capability dominates machinery. Holding the most reliable architecture fixed, the only factor that moved the frontier was the capability of the base model. The stronger model does not win by classifying differently; it wins by responding more cleanly within each category — shorter, more decisive refusals with less hedging and less paraphrase of the participant, exactly the surface on which the weaker model failed. Reasoning effort, escalated from low to high, never helped; an explicit correction stage was actively harmful.

Open caveat — judge independence

In the headline upgrade condition the chatbot and the judge are currently the same model, so a small self-leniency artefact cannot be excluded. The improvement is large, appears on factual accuracy as well, and tracks measurable changes in response length — all consistent with a genuine capability gain rather than judge complaisance. A confirmation condition that fully separates the evaluated and evaluating models is in progress. Until it lands, we state the finding as “base-model capability is the binding determinant of reliability,” not as a final production recommendation.

Implications for deployment

For a chatbot required to behave in a narrowly prescribed way, reliability is governed by the capability of the base model rather than the elaborateness of the surrounding workflow. A verification or correction layer should not be added to a sequential conversational assistant without direct evidence that it improves adherence on the responses it touches; compute is better spent on a stronger base model, concentrated on the clarification and example categories where residual violations live. Because a small per-response violation probability still contaminates most fifteen-turn conversations, reliability should be reported per conversation, not per response.

Methods & reproducibility

Procedure

A subject → chatbot → judge loop with per-turn tracing. Architectures, subject types, scored dimensions, and model assignments are all JSON-configurable.

Statistics

Violation rates with Wilson 95% CIs at two scales: per response and per conversation (“at least one violation in fifteen turns”). Accumulation modelled as near-independent Bernoulli draws.

Data

More than twenty-seven thousand independently scored responses and counting. The aggregates on this page are regenerated from the raw logs by a single script and committed alongside the site.
